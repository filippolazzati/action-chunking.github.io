Why Does Action Chunking Improve Behavioral Cloning Performance in Robotic Control?

Abstract

Action chunking—predicting and executing multiple future actions instead of a single action—has proven to be a critical component for learning effective robotic control policies. However, our precise understanding of why action chunking improves performance has remained limited. In this work we seek to close this gap. Through rigorous experimental evaluations, we first show that, while existing hypotheses for the success of action chunking—in particular non-Markovian expressivity, horizon reduction, and representation learning—can each partially explain the success of action chunking, in many settings of interest delayed policies, which at each step predict a single action based on the state \(k\) steps in the past, can match or exceed the performance of action chunking, fully accounting for the benefits of these hypotheses. We then show that there exists an additional benefit of action chunking not accounted for previously that we refer to as implicit ensembling. In particular, by learning a diversity of temporal relationships (that is, \(a_t \mid o_t\), \(a_t \mid o_{t-1}\), \(\ldots\)), action-chunked policies exhibit behavior matching that of a model ensemble, increasing their robustness and generalization ability over policies that only learn a single temporal relationship. Motivated by these findings, we propose a policy class that amplifies this effect of action chunking by explicitly instantiating an ensemble, and which we show significantly improves over the performance of action chunking in many domains.

Section 0

Setting

Our focus is on the Robomimic and Libero simulation benchmarks, and on the Droid setup on the Franka real robot. These problems share the following structure:
  • Low control frequencies (15-20Hz)
  • Few demonstrations datasets (50-300 demos per task)
  • Delta position control (either EE or joint)
  • Overparameterized diffusion policies
We leave to future works the task of assessing whether our insights generalize also to other settings (eg, high control frequencies, large demonstration datasets).

Section 1

Hypothesis 1: Expressivity

This hypothesis posits that action chunking policies are more expressive (non-Markovian) than common Markovian policies learned with Behavioral Cloning, and so better capture non-Markov human behaviors.

Of course, a pre-condition for this hypothesis is that humans exhibit some sort of non-Markov behavior. We noted that the human demonstrations provided in the Libero benchmark contain some non-Markovian behavior: pauses when the arm interacts with objects. To see this, consider the three images below, which are taken from an expert demonstration for the task of opening the bottom drawer. Intuitively, at t=80, due to the high control frequency (20Hz), the arm gets stuck for a few frames (~5) before actually moving to the right.


To test this hypothesis, we compared the validation loss of a Markovian learner $a_t|s_t$ with that of a delayed policy $a_t| s_{t-n}$, where both of them are obtained from the same action chunking trained policy $a_{t:t+20}|s_t$.

As you can see from the figure on the left below, the validation loss does not increase (on average over all Libero 90 tasks) as we increase the delay $n$, but it even reduces a bit! While there might be various explanations for this phenomenon, we found that, for some specific tasks and specific expert demonstrations (especially when the validation trajectory is close to some trajectory observed during training), the difference is much more evident when the demonstrator pauses, ie, it exhibits some non-Markovian behavior. For instance, as you can see from the figure on the right below, which corresponds to the "open the bottom drawer" task mentioned earlier, we see that the delayed policy has lower loss aroung timestep 80, which corresponds to the pause.
But how do delayed policies perform in terms of success rate? As you can see from the figure below on the left, they perform much better than the Markovian learner, matching (or even exceeding) action chunking in Libero 90.

One question that arises is then: "Is the improvement in success rate due to the (slight) improvement in validation loss?" To test this, we plotted on the figure on the right below, for each of the libero 90 tasks, its difference in success rate between action chunking and Markovian policies, versus the difference in validation loss between the Markovian policy and the best (non-Markovian) delayed policy. Intuitively, if Hypothesis 1 was the most relevant, then we would expect to see a positive correlation, because that would mean that in tasks where delayed policies exhibit lower validation loss (ie, the expert is more non-Markovian), then action chunking performs better. However, as you can see from this figure, this is not the case (there are even points with negative difference in validation loss for which action chunking performs better).

Takeaway 1

Expressivity alone does not explain action chunking gains

Section 2

Hypothesis 2: Horizon Reduction

One of the core and well-known issues of Behavioral Cloning (and imitation learning in general) is that it suffers from error compounding, that is, making mistakes increases our odds of making additional mistakes as mistakes tend to bring us in unseen states. As a consequence, Markovian learners suffer from exponentially worse performance in the worst case, as shown by the following theorem.

Theoretical result

Lower bound for Markovian behavioral cloning

There exists a deterministic environment with horizon $H$, a Markov demonstrator $\pi_{\mathrm{demo}}$, and a Markov policy $\hat{\pi}$ with generalization error $\epsilon$, such that $\mathcal{J}(\pi_{\mathrm{demo}})\ge \mathcal{J}(\hat{\pi})+\Omega(2^H \epsilon)$.

Hypothesis 2 posits that action chunks mitigate compounding error by reducing the effective horizon: informally, the intuition is that, by taking $H/k$ actions instead of $H$ actions (where $H$ is the horizon and $k$ the chunk size), the exponent in the result above should be divided by $k$. As a premise to this hypothesis, is that action chunking is more sample efficient than Markovian behavioral cloning. To test this, we trained diffusion policies for increasing amounts of demonstrations in Libero 90. As you can see in the figure below, the gap between action chunking and the Markovian learner is much larger with 10 demonstrations than with 50, supporting the claim that action chunking is more sample efficient.
However, the simulation above shows also that delayed policies are more sample efficient than Markov policies, and with similar success rates to action chunking policies. Since delayed policies take actions at every step (ie, they do not reduce the horizon of the problem), then there should be another reason accumunating both action chunking and delayed policies, that justifies their improved sample efficiency. Interestingly, we found that, \emph{if we make predictions based on past states, then we reduce compounding errors when the dynamics is smooth, because past states should be closer to distribution than the current states (in the worst case)}. This intuition is formalized in the following theoretical result.

Theoretical result

Upper bound for action chunking and delayed behavioral cloning

Suppose that the generalization error of each action chunk component/delayed policy is no larger than that of a Markovian learner, and is bounded by $\epsilon$. Then both the action-chunked and delayed policies satisfy $\mathcal{J}(\pi_{\rm demo})-\mathcal{J}(\hat\pi^k_n),\; \mathcal{J}(\pi_{\rm demo})-\mathcal{J}(\pi_{\rm delay}^k[\hat\pi_n]) \le \mathcal{O}((k+1)^{H/k}\epsilon)$, where $k$ is the chunk size/delay.

Takeaway 2

Action chunking reduces compounding error by predicting on past states

Section 4

Hypothesis 3: Representation Learning

A commonly observed phenomenon is that computing a Markovian learner by first training an action chunking policy with chunk size $>1$, and then playing only the first action, improves and sometimes matches the performance of action chunking. To test if just using action chunking in this way during training is the key, we compared the following four methods in Libero 90:
  • Markovian learner
  • Markovian induced by a chunked-trained policy
  • Delayed policy
  • Delayed policy induced by a chunked-trained policy

Action chunking seems to give representational benefits for short horizons only

Section 5

Implicit Ensembles

The preceding results suggest that non-Markovian expressivity and reduction in compounding error not only contribute to the success of action chunking, but also that, on Libero, we can match the performance of action chunking with only a delayed policy.

However, these results do not fully transfer to Robomimic (or our real-world setup, see next section), as delayed policies alone are not able to match the performance of action, as shown in the next plot (results are averaged over four Robomimic tasks).
Our key insight is that action chunking works, implicitly, as an ensemble of delayed policies. To see this, think to how random forests work in supervised learning: Given a training dataset $\mathcal{D}=\{(x_1,x_2,\dotsc,x_d,y)\}$, where $x_i$ are different features and $y$ is the target, one way to create an ensemble of learners is to make sure that each learner is trained on different features, so, say, train $d$ learners each to model a different relationship $x_i\to y$. Then, at inference, aggregate the predictions of all these learners.

Our insight is that, if we think to each feature $x_i$ as a different past state $s_{t-i}$, and we realize that predictions from different learners can be aggregated through time by playing them at difference timesteps, then also action chunking works in this way.

To test this, we first tested that an ensemble of different past states achieves indeed lower generalization error. As you can see from the figure below, AC$(n)$-TE, which aggregates predictions from different delayed policies simply by computing the average, achieves lower validation error than a Markovian learner and any delayed policy considered, supporting our thesis. Moreover, this picture also shows that also an explicit ensemble of delayed policies achieves lower validation error in Robomimic.
To further validate this implicit ensembles hypothesis, we also compared the success rate of AC$(n)$-TE with that of action chunking and that of the policy which, at each timestep, selects at random which delayed policy to use (so to replicate the implicit ensemble through time of action chunking with a different order with which playing delayed policies). As you can see from the table below, the performance of these methods match, supporting our claims.
